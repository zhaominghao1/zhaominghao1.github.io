Writing

    1. College Admissions and Replicator Dynamics (UCSD MATH_111A)

    We examined the college admission process through mathematical modeling and game theory and explored how colleges dynamically adapt their admission strategies over time based on the outcomes of previous application cycles. We applied replicator dynamics to capture the evolu- tion of strategic preferences in a competitive environment. The model is governed by a system of ordinary differential equations, where the frequency of a college's strategy increases if its payoff exceeds the average. We analyze the resulting dynamics and investigate conditions under which strategies stabilize or fluctuate. This framework provides insight into how institutional behavior in college admissions may shift over time in response to outcomes from earlier cycles. Our findings suggest that even in a simplified system, dynamic strategy adjustment can lead to nontrivial long-term patterns that mirror real world behavior.

    2. An Empirical Study of Monetary Policy on Wyoming State (UCSD ECON_112)

    In this project, we utilized Time-Series metrics to determine whether the causa relationship between monetary policy and economic activity indicatiors. We plotted the impulse response functions of the Federal Funds Rate and growth rate of real GDP and unemployment rate respectively. In the end we deduced a positive causal effect on GDP growth and a negative one on unemployment, which is consistent with the predictions from Macroeconomic models.

    3. US Total Wealth Prediction (UCSD ECON_178)

    We utilized Machine Learning Techniques to predict the total wealth of US individuals using a representative sample. The models ranges from linear regression, ridge regression, forward/backward stepwise selection, Lasso and polynomial regession and were implemented in R. We selected the model that has the highest predictive accuracy using cross validation and comparing the predictive error.
